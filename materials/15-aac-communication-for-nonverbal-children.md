# AAC and Communication for Nonverbal and Minimally Verbal Autistic Children

## What Is AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication)

AAC refers to any method of communication that supplements or replaces speech. This includes pictures, symbol boards, sign language, speech-generating devices, and communication apps. AAC is for children who cannot reliably use speech to communicate their wants, needs, thoughts, and feelings.

AAC is not a last resort — it is a powerful, evidence-based tool that improves quality of life, reduces frustration and meltdowns, and supports relationships. The sooner a child has a reliable way to communicate, the better their outcomes.

**Who uses AAC:** Children who are nonverbal, minimally verbal (fewer than 20 functional words), or who lose speech during stress. AAC can be used alongside speech, not instead of it.

## The Most Important Myth to Dispel: AAC Does NOT Stop Speech Development

This is the most common fear parents and even some professionals have. The research is clear: AAC does not reduce speech. Multiple studies — including a major 2006 meta-analysis by Millar, Light, and Schlosser — show that AAC consistently **increases** vocalization and spoken language in children with autism. AAC reduces communication frustration, which is a major barrier to speech development.

If a speech therapist or teacher tells you that using pictures or a device will stop your child from talking, that is not supported by evidence. You have the right to request a second opinion.

## Types of AAC: No-Tech, Low-Tech, and High-Tech

**No-tech AAC:**
- Sign language (ASL or simplified sign systems like Makaton)
- Gestures and pointing
- Eye gaze (looking at a desired object)

**Low-tech AAC:**
- PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System) — pictures on cards
- Communication books with symbols
- Choice boards (2–4 picture choices)
- First-Then boards
- Visual schedules

**High-tech AAC:**
- Speech-generating devices (SGDs): dedicated devices that speak for the child
- AAC apps on iPad or tablet: Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, LAMP Words for Life, Snap Core First
- Eye-gaze technology for children with motor limitations

## PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System) — Stage by Stage

PECS was developed by Andy Bondy and Lori Frost. It teaches communication through picture exchange in six stages:

**Phase 1 — How to Communicate:** Child learns to pick up a picture and hand it to a communication partner to get a desired item. Requires two adults initially — one behind the child to prompt, one to receive the picture.

**Phase 2 — Distance and Persistence:** Child learns to travel to find their communication partner and give the picture even when the partner is not right next to them.

**Phase 3 — Picture Discrimination:** Child learns to choose between two or more pictures to request the correct item. This is where the picture book becomes essential.

**Phase 4 — Sentence Structure:** Child uses a sentence strip: "I want ___." They place the "I want" card and their chosen picture on the strip and hand it to the partner.

**Phase 5 — Responding to Questions:** Child learns to respond to "What do you want?" using PECS.

**Phase 6 — Commenting:** Child learns to use PECS to comment on the environment, not just request: "I see ___," "I have ___."

PECS training should be done with support from a speech-language pathologist (SLP) familiar with the system. Free introductory materials are available at pecsusa.com.

## Speech-Generating Devices (SGDs)

SGDs are dedicated electronic devices that produce speech when the child touches symbols, pictures, or text. They are powerful because they give the child a voice anywhere.

**Common SGDs and apps:**

- **Proloquo2Go** (iPad app, $250): One of the most widely used AAC apps. Symbol-based, customizable, supports multiple languages. Used in many schools.
- **TouchChat HD** (iPad app, $150–$300): Similar to Proloquo2Go. Supports many vocabulary sets.
- **LAMP Words for Life** (iPad app): Based on motor learning — consistent motor patterns for words. Often recommended for young or newly AAC users.
- **Snap Core First** (app and dedicated device): Good for beginners; robust symbol library.
- **Dedicated SGDs** (Tobii Dynavox, PRC Saltillo): More durable devices. Often funded through Medicaid or insurance. Can cost $3,000–$8,000 but are fully covered with proper documentation.

## Free and Low-Cost AAC Options

For families who cannot afford paid apps:

- **Cboard** (cboard.io): Free, open-source, web-based AAC. No download required. Supports many languages.
- **LetMeTalk** (Android, free): Simple symbol-based AAC app.
- **Snap Scene** (free tier available): Photo-based communication.
- **Google Slides or PowerPoint:** Parents can create custom low-tech communication boards for free using Google Images and print them.
- **Boardmaker Share** (boardmakershare.com): Free premade symbol boards.
- **TalkBox** or similar apps: Some states provide free devices through loan programs — ask your Regional Center or state AT program.

## How to Get an AAC Device Funded

**Through Medicaid:** Medicaid covers SGDs as "durable medical equipment" when a physician and SLP document medical necessity. You need:
1. A prescription from the child's doctor
2. An AAC evaluation from a licensed SLP
3. A letter of medical necessity
4. Prior authorization from Medicaid

This process takes weeks to months but results in a fully covered device. Do not give up if denied — appeal.

**Through school (IDEA):** If the IEP team determines that AAC is required for the child to access education, the school must provide it at no cost. Request an assistive technology evaluation through the IEP process. Write: "I request an AT evaluation to determine if my child needs AAC to access their education."

**Through insurance:** Private insurance varies. AAC is often covered under speech therapy benefits or durable medical equipment. Ask for an itemized denial in writing if refused — you can appeal.

**Through nonprofits:** Organizations like the AAC Institute (aacinstitute.org) and Communication First (communicationfirst.org) sometimes provide devices or funding information.

## Core Vocabulary vs. Fringe Vocabulary

**Core vocabulary** = the small set of words used most frequently across all contexts. Words like: want, more, stop, help, go, no, yes, like, I, you, it, that, good, bad, feel, make, put. These 200–400 words make up 80% of what we say.

**Fringe vocabulary** = specific words unique to an individual (names of toys, foods, family members).

For AAC, start with core vocabulary. A child who can say "want," "stop," "help," and "more" can communicate in almost any situation. Apps like Proloquo2Go and LAMP are organized around core vocabulary.

## How to Model AAC at Home (Aided Language Stimulation)

The most important thing parents can do: **model using the AAC system yourself.** Touch the symbols on the device while you speak. Every time you say a word that is on the device, also touch it.

Example: Child looks at a cookie. You touch "want" then "cookie" on the device and say "want cookie." You are not asking the child to do anything — you are showing them how the system works.

Children who receive consistent AAC modeling from parents begin using the system faster. Model at least 30–50 times per day. Make it part of meals, play, and routines.

Do not wait for the child to imitate before you model. Just model consistently.

## Making Communication Boards at Home for Free

You do not need a device to start AAC. A homemade communication board works:

1. Decide on 4–8 words or pictures relevant to your child's life
2. Search Google Images or use Boardmaker symbols (boardmakershare.com has free ones)
3. Print, laminate (or tape over with packing tape), and attach velcro
4. Use consistently throughout the day

Start with a simple choice board: 2 pictures of things the child loves. Hold it out and wait. Any touch or look counts as a response. Reward immediately.

## What to Say at an IEP Meeting to Get AAC

Write and say:
- "I request an assistive technology evaluation to determine if my child needs AAC."
- "My child does not have a reliable way to communicate. This is affecting their access to education."
- "I am requesting that AAC assessment be completed within 60 days."

Schools are required to conduct this evaluation at no cost. If they refuse, file a state complaint or contact your Parent Training and Information (PTI) center — find yours at parentcenterhub.org.
