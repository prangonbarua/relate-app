# Safety, Wandering Prevention, and Parent Self-Care

## Why Autistic Children Wander (Elope)

Wandering — also called elopement — is when a child leaves a safe, supervised area without permission or awareness of danger. It is one of the most serious safety concerns for autism families.

**The statistics:**
- 49% of autistic children have wandered at least once after age 4 (National Autism Association, 2012)
- Nearly 1 in 3 autistic children who elope are in danger of traffic injury
- Drowning is the #1 cause of death from elopement — accounting for 71–91% of lethal cases (NAA Big Red Safety Box data)
- Children with autism are 160 times more likely to drown than neurotypical children

**Why it happens:**
- Children may be attracted to water, open spaces, or specific locations (a store, a park, a neighbor's house)
- They may not understand danger or traffic
- They may be fleeing sensory overload or a stressful situation
- They may not respond to their name being called
- It can happen in seconds, even to attentive parents — this is not negligence

## Immediate Home Safety Steps

**Doors and windows:**
- Install door alarms (simple battery-powered alarms that beep when a door opens — under $20 at hardware stores)
- Add secondary locks high on doors (deadbolts or chain locks that a child cannot reach)
- Consider a door alarm with a chime that wakes you at night
- Install window guards or window stops on all reachable windows
- Smart locks with codes can prevent a child from leaving while you are in another room

**Fencing:**
- If you have a yard, a 6-foot fence with self-latching gates is essential
- Pool fencing: If there is any pool or body of water nearby, it must have a 4-sided, self-latching fence (this is law in many states)

**Bedroom:**
- Some families use a door alarm or baby monitor on the child's bedroom door for night safety

**Cost:** Many of these items cost $15–$50. If cost is a barrier, the National Autism Association (nationalautismassociation.org) provides free safety kits through their Big Red Safety Box program — apply online.

## Identification Options

Every autistic child who wanders should have identification on them at all times:

- **Medical alert bracelet or ID tag:** Include child's name, diagnosis ("autism, nonverbal"), and a parent's phone number. Available on Amazon or at pharmacies for $10–$30.
- **Shoe ID tags:** Small tags that attach inside a shoe with contact information
- **Iron-on labels:** Iron into clothing with name and phone number
- **Temporary tattoos:** ID tattoos with contact information that last several days — used during trips or outings (SafetyTat.com)
- **GPS trackers:** Wearable GPS devices (AngelSense, Jiobit, Apple Watch with Family Setup) allow real-time location tracking. AngelSense is specifically designed for autistic children and has a listen-in feature.

## Register with Local Police and First Responders

- **Take your child to the local police station** and provide a photo, description, and information about their autism (nonverbal, attracted to water, responds to name, does not respond to name, etc.)
- **Project Lifesaver** (projectlifesaver.org): A program where trained officers use tracking equipment to locate individuals who wander. Free to join. Available in many counties.
- **Special Needs Registry / Autism Registry:** Many police departments and 911 systems have voluntary registries. When you call 911 to report a missing child, they flag your address immediately. Call your local non-emergency police line to ask if this exists in your area.
- **Provide your child's photo and information** to neighbors, schools, and anyone who might encounter your child

## Water Safety

Because drowning is the leading cause of death in autism elopement:
- Enroll your child in water safety swimming lessons as early as possible — even if they love water. Knowing how to float and reach the wall saves lives.
- Many YMCAs offer adapted swimming lessons for children with disabilities. Cost varies; scholarships are available.
- **Never leave a child with autism unsupervised near any water** — pool, pond, bathtub, irrigation ditch, or open bucket of water
- If you live near water, consider a wearable life vest for backyard time

## If Your Child Goes Missing

1. **Call 911 immediately** — do not wait. Tell them your child has autism and whether they are verbal.
2. Describe: clothing, hair color, height, weight, any ID they are wearing, where they were last seen, and what they might be attracted to (water, a specific place)
3. **Check water first** — pools, ponds, storm drains, fountains
4. Ask neighbors to check their yards, garages, sheds, and cars immediately
5. Activate any GPS tracker your child wears
6. Post on local Facebook groups and Nextdoor immediately — community searches work
7. **Do not assume your child is hiding nearby** — autistic children can cover significant distances quickly

## The Reality of Caregiver Burnout

Raising an autistic child is one of the most demanding parenting experiences. The research is clear:

- Parents of autistic children report **3x higher rates of depression** than parents of neurotypical children (Benson, 2006)
- Mothers of autistic children experience chronic stress levels comparable to combat veterans (Seltzer et al., 2010)
- Immigrant parents face an additional layer: **language barriers, cultural isolation, lack of family support network, fear of systems, and stigma** within their own community

This is not weakness. This is the normal response to an abnormal level of demand. Acknowledging burnout is not giving up — it is the first step to getting support.

## Signs of Burnout vs. Normal Tiredness

Normal tiredness improves with rest. Burnout does not.

Signs of burnout:
- Feeling emotionally numb or detached from your child or partner
- Constant irritability or anger that feels out of proportion
- Physical exhaustion that sleep doesn't fix
- Feeling hopeless about the future
- Withdrawing from friends, family, or activities you used to enjoy
- Feeling like you are "just surviving," not living
- Intrusive thoughts about what would happen if you weren't there

If you have thoughts of harming yourself: Call 988 immediately. You matter. Your child needs you alive and present.

## Respite Care: What It Is and How to Get It

Respite care is temporary relief for caregivers — someone trained to care for your child so you can rest, attend appointments, or spend time with your partner or other children.

**How to access respite:**
- **Regional Center (California and most states):** Regional Centers fund respite hours for families of children with developmental disabilities. Contact your Regional Center service coordinator and ask for "respite services." This is free for eligible families.
- **Medicaid Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) Waiver:** Most states have a Medicaid waiver that funds respite care. There are often waitlists — get on the list now even if you don't feel you need it yet.
- **Autism Society of America:** Can connect you with local respite programs. autism-society.org
- **ARCH National Respite Network:** archrespite.org — searchable national database of respite providers
- **YMCA and local nonprofits:** Many offer drop-in childcare programs for children with disabilities

Ask your pediatrician, school, or IEP team for referrals to local respite services.

## Online Support Communities for Immigrant Parents

You do not need to be alone with this. Community helps:

- **Autism Society of America local chapters:** autism-society.org/chapters — find a local chapter near you, many have multilingual support
- **Facebook groups:** Search for "autism parents [your language]" — e.g., "padres de niños con autismo," "자폐 부모 모임," "autisme parents francophones"
- **Reddit:** r/autism and r/Autism_Parenting have large, supportive communities
- **GRASP (Global Regional Autism Spectrum Partnership):** grasp.org — support for autistic people and their families
- **Undivided:** undivided.io — an app specifically for special needs families to navigate systems

## Mental Health Resources for Parents

- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text 988. Available 24/7 in Spanish and English. Online chat also available. Free.
- **NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness):** 1-800-950-6264 | nami.org — also has family support groups
- **Open Path Collective:** openpathcollective.org — sliding scale therapy ($30–$80/session) for families who cannot afford standard rates
- **Therapy for immigrant families:** Search "culturally competent therapist" + your language or community on Psychology Today (therapist directory with insurance/sliding scale filters)
- **Your child's Regional Center:** Can sometimes fund parent counseling as a "parent training" service

## Talking to Family Who Don't Understand Autism

Many immigrant families face pressure from grandparents, aunts and uncles, or community members who believe autism is caused by vaccines, bad parenting, the evil eye, punishment for sin, or can be cured by prayer alone. This causes enormous stress.

Suggested approaches:
- Share a short video in your language from a medical source (Autism Speaks has resources in 12+ languages)
- Frame it as a medical condition: "His brain is wired differently, like someone might have a heart condition"
- Set boundaries kindly: "I know you love us. What I need from you right now is [specific help], not advice about causes or cures"
- Invite family to attend one therapy session or school visit so they see the work your child is doing
- You do not need to convince everyone. Focus on the people who are willing to learn.

## Self-Compassion: You Are Doing Enough

Research on parent wellbeing consistently shows that self-compassion — treating yourself with the same kindness you would give a friend — is one of the strongest predictors of caregiver resilience.

You do not have to be the perfect parent. You do not have to attend every therapy session, execute every strategy perfectly, or know all the answers. What your child needs most is a parent who is present, regulated, and loving — not a perfect therapist.

Rest is not selfish. Getting support is not weakness. You cannot pour from an empty cup.
