# 1. FEDERAL PROGRAMS

## 1.1 IDEA - Individuals with Disabilities Education Act

- **Website:** [U.S. Department of Education IDEA Page](https://www.ed.gov/laws-and-policy/individuals-disabilities/idea)
- **OSEP Website:** [Office of Special Education Programs](https://www.ed.gov/about/ed-offices/osers/osep)
- **Congressional Research:** [IDEA Part B](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R41833) | [IDEA Part C](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R43631)

IDEA is the primary federal law governing special education and early intervention services. It guarantees all children with disabilities, including autism, the right to a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE).

**CRITICAL FOR IMMIGRANT FAMILIES:** IDEA services are available to ALL children regardless of immigration status. Under the Supreme Court's 1982 *Plyler v. Doe* decision, undocumented children have the same right to public education (including special education) as any other resident. Schools CANNOT ask about immigration status.

### Part C - Early Intervention (Birth to Age 3)

- **Who it serves:** Infants and toddlers (birth through age 2) with developmental delays or disabilities, including autism
- **What it provides:** Speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, behavioral support, assistive technology, family training
- **Cost:** Services are provided at no cost or low cost to families (varies by state); evaluations are always free
- **How to access:** Contact your state's Part C early intervention program (see State-by-State Directory below)
- **Key document:** Each child receives an Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP)
- **FY2025 Funding:** $540 million in federal grants
- **ECTA Center Part C Coordinators by State:** https://ectacenter.org/contact/ptccoord.asp

### Part B - Special Education (Ages 3-21)

- **Who it serves:** Children and youth ages 3 through 21 with disabilities (autism is one of 14 qualifying categories)
- **What it provides:** Special education and related services through an Individualized Education Program (IEP)
- **Cost:** Completely free (FAPE)
- **How to access:** Request an evaluation from your child's school district
- **FY2025 Funding:** $420 million for preschool grants (Section 619)

## 1.2 Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act

- **Website:** [U.S. Department of Education Section 504](https://www.ed.gov/laws-and-policy/individuals-disabilities/section-504)
- **Wrightslaw Guide:** [Section 504 Resources](https://www.wrightslaw.com/info/sec504.index.htm)
- **AANE Guide:** [Section 504 History and Application](https://aane.org/autism-info-faqs/library/section-504-of-the-rehabilitation-act-the-power-of-its-history-application/)

Section 504 is a civil rights law that prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in any program receiving federal financial assistance. Children with autism are protected under Section 504.

**Key protections:**
- Schools must provide a "504 Plan" with accommodations (extra test time, preferred seating, sensory breaks, modified assignments)
- Broader eligibility than IDEA -- a child who doesn't qualify for an IEP may still qualify for a 504 Plan
- Covers any student with a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities

---

## 1.3 ADA - Americans with Disabilities Act

The ADA extends Section 504's protections to all public accommodations (restaurants, stores, parks), state and local government services, employment, transportation, and telecommunications. If your child is covered by Section 504, ADA protections also apply. Public parks, recreation programs, summer camps, and community activities must provide reasonable accommodations for children with autism.

## 1.4 Medicaid

### EPSDT - Early and Periodic Screening, Diagnostic, and Treatment

- **Website:** [Medicaid.gov EPSDT](https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/benefits/early-and-periodic-screening-diagnostic-and-treatment/index.html)
- **Coverage Guide (PDF):** [EPSDT Coverage Guide](https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/benefits/downloads/epsdt-coverage-guide.pdf)
- **Autism Services:** [Medicaid.gov Autism Services](https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/benefits/autism-services)
- **Autism Speaks EPSDT Guide:** [Autism Speaks Medicaid EPSDT](https://www.autismspeaks.org/medicaid-epsdt)

EPSDT is the child health component of Medicaid and is the most comprehensive benefit available for children under 21 enrolled in Medicaid.

**What EPSDT covers for autism:**
- Autism screening at 18-month and 24-month well-child visits per AAP guidelines
- Diagnostic evaluation for autism
- ALL medically necessary treatment services, including: Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) therapy, speech and language therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, psychological/behavioral services, prescription medications, assistive technology and communication devices

**Critical point:** Under EPSDT, states MUST provide any Medicaid-coverable service that is medically necessary to correct or ameliorate a child's condition, even if the service is not normally covered in the state's Medicaid plan. As of 2022, all 50 states have implemented Medicaid coverage for autism treatment.

### Medicaid HCBS Waivers for Autism

- **Federal Waiver List:** [Medicaid.gov Waiver List](https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/section-1115-demo/demonstration-and-waiver-list)
- **Kids' Waivers Directory:** [KidsWaivers.org](https://www.kidswaivers.org/)
- **Full State List:** [KidsWaivers.org Full List](https://www.kidswaivers.org/full-list/)

**Key facts:**
- 28 states + DC have current or former HCBS waivers that include children with ASD
- Services vary by state but may include: ABA therapy, respite care, personal care, residential habilitation, day programs, employment support, family training, assistive technology
- Many states have LONG waitlists (national average: 39 months as of FY2018)
- States with autism-specific waivers include: Colorado, Indiana, Maryland, Massachusetts, Wisconsin, and others

## 1.5 CHIP - Children's Health Insurance Program

- **Website:** [Medicaid.gov CHIP](https://www.medicaid.gov/chip)
- **Eligibility:** [HealthCare.gov CHIP](https://www.healthcare.gov/medicaid-chip/childrens-health-insurance-program/)
- **Autism Speaks Guide:** [Autism Speaks CHIP](https://www.autismspeaks.org/childrens-health-insurance-program)
- **Apply:** Call **1-877-KIDS-NOW (1-877-543-7669)** or visit InsureKidsNow.gov

CHIP provides health insurance for children in families who earn too much for Medicaid but cannot afford private insurance. Autism coverage varies significantly by state. CHIP programs structured as Medicaid expansions must provide EPSDT benefits. Separate CHIP programs may have more limited benefits. Some states (e.g., Texas) do NOT cover autism-specific services under CHIP.

## 1.6 SSI - Supplemental Security Income for Children with Autism

- **Website:** [SSA.gov SSI for Children](https://www.ssa.gov/ssi/text-child-ussi.htm)
- **Benefits for Children with Disabilities (2026 PDF):** [SSA Publication](https://www.ssa.gov/pubs/EN-05-10026.pdf)
- **Phone:** **1-800-772-1213** (TTY: 1-800-325-0778)
- **Additional guidance:** [Special Needs Answers SSI Guide](https://specialneedsanswers.com/supplemental-security-income-ssi-for-children-with-autism-19640)

SSI provides monthly cash payments to children with disabilities in low-income families.

### Eligibility:

**Medical criteria:**
- Child must have a medically determinable physical or mental impairment
- Must result in marked and severe functional limitations lasting at least 12 months
- Autism is listed in SSA Blue Book: Section 112.10 (children) and Section 12.10 (adults)
- Required documentation: Qualitative deficits in verbal/nonverbal communication and social interaction AND significantly restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior

**Financial criteria:**
- Based on household income and assets (parents' income counts for children under 18)
- Asset limit: $2,000 for an individual

### Benefit Amount (2025):
- Maximum federal monthly benefit: **$967/month**
- Some states provide additional supplemental payments
- Amount may be reduced based on family income

### Application Process:
1. Call SSA at **1-800-772-1213** to schedule an appointment
2. Gather documentation: medical records, doctor's notes, IEP/school records, therapy records
3. Provide identifying information, tax documents, financial information
4. SSA will review medical evidence and may request a Consultative Exam
5. Initial processing: 3-6 months

**Important for autism cases:** Many ASD claims are initially denied because medical records focus on diagnosis rather than functional limitations. During SSA's Consultative Exam, individuals with autism may "mask" or over-perform socially, leading to denials. Document specific functional limitations in daily activities.

**Medicaid link:** In most states, children who receive SSI are automatically eligible for Medicaid (accessing EPSDT benefits).

## 1.7 Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI)

- **Website:** https://www.ssa.gov/disability
- **Phone:** 1-800-772-1213
- **Guide:** [OAR Guide to SSDI for Autism](https://researchautism.org/blog/applying-for-social-security-disability-benefits-with-autism/)
- **PA Guide:** [PAAutism Social Security](https://paautism.org/resource/social-security/)

SSDI is primarily for adults with disabilities who have sufficient work history. Most adults with autism apply for SSI rather than SSDI. Key exception: **Adult Disabled Child (DAC) benefit** -- adults with autism diagnosed before age 22 may qualify for SSDI based on a parent's work record if the parent is deceased, retired, or receiving disability. 2025 maximum SSDI benefit: $4,018/month (average: ~$1,580/month).

## 1.8 Vocational Rehabilitation (VR) Services

- **Governing Agency:** Rehabilitation Services Administration (RSA), U.S. Department of Education
- **Website:** https://rsa.ed.gov/about/programs
- **TACAN Guide:** [TACAN VR Resources](https://tacanow.org/family-resources/vocational-rehabilitation/)
- **IACC Transition Resources:** [IACC Transition](https://iacc.hhs.gov/resources/transition/)

VR is a federally funded, state-administered program helping individuals with disabilities prepare for, find, and maintain employment.

**Services for individuals with autism:**
- Vocational assessment and counseling
- Job placement assistance
- On-the-job training and support
- Pre-Employment Transition Services (Pre-ETS) for students ages 14-22: job exploration counseling, work-based learning, workplace readiness training, postsecondary education counseling, self-advocacy instruction
- Supported employment services
- Assistive technology

**How to access:** Self-refer by contacting your state's VR agency, or request a referral through school, healthcare provider, or community organization. Employment outcomes: approximately 50-60% of transition-age youth with autism are employed when they exit VR.

# 2. SCHOOL RESOURCES AND EDUCATIONAL RIGHTS

## 2.1 IEP Process (Simplified for Parents)

- **Parent Center Hub:** [IEP Overview](https://www.parentcenterhub.org/iep-overview/)
- **Wrightslaw:** [Special Education Law](https://www.wrightslaw.com/)
- **FAPE Guide:** [Understanding FAPE](https://www.understood.org/en/articles/what-is-and-isnt-covered-under-fape)

### What is an IEP?
A legally binding document describing the specialized instruction, services, and supports a child with a disability will receive in school. It is FREE.

### Step-by-Step Process:

**Step 1 - Request Evaluation:** Write a letter to the school principal or special education director requesting a full evaluation. Include your child's name, DOB, grade, and your concerns. Keep a copy. Send by certified mail or hand-deliver.

**Step 2 - Evaluation:** FREE to the family. Must be completed within 60 days of parental consent (or state timeline). Parents must provide written consent before evaluation begins.

**Step 3 - Eligibility Determination:** A team including parents reviews results and determines if the child qualifies under one of 14 IDEA categories (autism is one).

**Step 4 - IEP Development:** Must be developed within 30 days of eligibility. Team includes parents, general ed teacher, special ed teacher, district representative, evaluation specialist, and (when appropriate) the child.

**Step 5 - Implementation:** Services begin as soon as possible. IEP reviewed annually; child re-evaluated every 3 years.

### Child Find Obligation:
Schools have a legal duty ("Child Find") to identify, locate, and evaluate ALL children suspected of having a disability, regardless of immigration status, English proficiency, enrollment status, or housing status.

**References:** [Requesting Evaluation](https://www.parentcenterhub.org/evaluation-2/) | [CHOP Evaluation Guide](https://www.research.chop.edu/car-autism-roadmap/school-age-special-education-evaluations) | [Child Find](https://wapave.org/child-find-schools-have-a-legal-duty-to-evaluate-children-impacted-by-disability/)

## 2.2 504 Plan

For students with disabilities who need accommodations but not specialized instruction. Common 504 accommodations for autism: extended time on tests, preferential seating, sensory breaks, modified homework, visual schedules, quiet testing location, advance notice of schedule changes, noise-canceling headphones.

## 2.3 Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)

- **Guide:** [Understood.org LRE](https://www.understood.org/en/articles/least-restrictive-environment-lre-what-you-need-to-know)
- **Wrightslaw:** [LRE Resources](https://www.wrightslaw.com/info/lre.index.htm)

IDEA requires students with disabilities be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Placement options range from general education classroom with supports through specialized programs.

## 2.4 Related Services Schools Must Provide

Under IDEA, for children with autism these may include: speech-language therapy (most common), occupational therapy (including sensory integration), physical therapy, Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA), psychological services/counseling, school health/nursing services, transportation, assistive technology (communication devices), parent counseling and training, social work services, and interpreter services (critical for non-English speaking families).

## 2.5 Dispute Resolution Options

- **Quick Guide (PDF):** [Dispute Resolution Comparison](https://cadreworks.org/sites/default/files/Dispute%20Resolution%20Process%20Comparison%20Chart.pdf)
- **Procedural Safeguards:** [PAVE Guide](https://wapave.org/procedural-safeguards-student-and-parent-rights-in-special-education/)

**Option 1 - Mediation:** Free, voluntary, confidential. Neutral mediator helps reach agreement. Faster than due process.

**Option 2 - State Complaint:** Written complaint to state education agency alleging IDEA violation. State investigates and issues decision within 60 days.

**Option 3 - Due Process Hearing:** Formal legal proceeding. Impartial hearing officer makes binding decision. Parents who prevail may be awarded attorney's fees.

**Parent Rights (Procedural Safeguards):** Right to participate in all meetings; examine all records; receive Prior Written Notice before changes; give or withhold consent; obtain Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense; receive information in your native language; bring advocates or attorneys to meetings.

## State Autism Insurance Mandates Summary

All 50 states require some form of autism/ABA coverage. Key variations:

**States with NO age limit and NO dollar cap (most comprehensive):** California, Connecticut, Maine, Massachusetts, Nevada, New York, Oregon, Vermont, and others.

**States with age limits:** Alabama (under 18), Arizona (under 17 for behavioral therapy), Georgia (6 and under), South Carolina (under 16), Virginia (2-10).

**States with dollar caps:** Alabama ($20K-$40K/yr by age), Arizona ($25K-$50K/yr), Florida ($36K/yr; $200K lifetime), Georgia ($35K/yr), Pennsylvania ($36K/yr), South Carolina ($50K/yr).

**Important:** The federal Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) may render many state-level dollar caps, visit limits, and age limits unenforceable.

**Full references:** [NCSL State Laws](https://www.ncsl.org/health/autism-and-insurance-coverage-state-laws) | [Autism Speaks State Plans](https://www.autismspeaks.org/state-regulated-health-benefit-plans) | [ABA Edu State Guide](https://www.appliedbehavioranalysisedu.org/state-by-state-guide-to-autism-insurance-laws/) | [Bierman Autism Insurance by State](https://www.biermanautism.com/resources/blog/autism-insurance-mandate-by-state/)

# 5. RESOURCES SPECIFICALLY FOR IMMIGRANT FAMILIES

## 5.1 Critical Legal Rights

**Education (ALL immigration statuses):**
- Under *Plyler v. Doe* (1982), ALL children have the right to free public education including special education
- Schools CANNOT ask about immigration status
- Schools MUST provide IDEA services (IEP, evaluations, related services) to all eligible children
- Schools MUST provide interpreter services and translated documents
- Reference: [ED Fact Sheet](https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/policy/rights/guid/unaccompanied-children.pdf) | [Alliance for Girls](https://www.alliance4girls.org/blog/voices/education-rights-of-immigrant-children-with-disabilities-in-public-schools)

**Medicaid/CHIP (varies by status):**
- Lawful permanent residents: Eligible after 5-year wait (some states cover children immediately)
- Refugees/asylees: Immediately eligible
- Undocumented: Emergency Medicaid only; WIC eligible
- 2025 Note: New federal restrictions imposed on immigrant Medicaid/CHIP eligibility (July 2025 budget reconciliation bill)
- Reference: [NILC Overview](https://www.nilc.org/resources/overview-immeligfedprograms/)

**SSI:** Must be US citizen or qualifying noncitizen.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR APP DEVELOPMENT

1. **IDEA services are available to ALL children regardless of immigration status** -- this is the single most important point for immigrant families. Schools cannot ask about immigration status.

2. **Early Intervention (birth-3) is the entry point** -- every state has a program and evaluation is always free. This should be the first action the app recommends.

3. **EPSDT under Medicaid is the most comprehensive benefit** -- it covers ALL medically necessary services for children under 21. Many families don't know this exists.

4. **Parent Training Centers exist in every state** and many serve families in multiple languages. They are free and provide one-on-one help.

5. **Waitlists are a major barrier** -- especially for Medicaid waivers (years-long waits in many states like Florida with 23,000+ on the waitlist). The app should help families get on waitlists early.

6. **The school system is a major free service provider** -- free evaluations, IEPs, speech therapy, OT, behavioral support. The app should guide families through requesting evaluations.

7. **Immigration status affects some benefits but NOT education** -- the app should clearly distinguish what is available regardless of status vs. what requires documentation.

8. **Spanish-language resources exist but are insufficient** -- the app fills a real gap, especially for non-Spanish immigrant languages.

9. **Navigation is the single biggest barrier** -- families don't know what exists or how to access it. The app's core value proposition should be simplifying this.

10. **Each state is different** -- the app MUST be state-aware to provide accurate information about specific programs, phone numbers, waivers, and insurance mandates.
